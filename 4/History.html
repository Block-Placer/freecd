History

Navigation has evolved through several groundbreaking milestones that changed how humans travel and explore. It began with the invention of the magnetic compass in ancient China around 200 BCE, which allowed sailors to determine direction even when the sky was cloudy or dark. Centuries later, in the 1760s, John Harrison’s marine chronometer solved the problem of finding longitude at sea, making long-distance voyages far safer and more accurate. These early tools laid the foundation for precise global navigation.

In the 1900s, technology advanced rapidly with the development of radio navigation systems like LORAN, helping ships and aircraft determine their positions. The next major leap came in 1978, when the United States launched the first GPS satellites, introducing space-based navigation. By the 2000s, several countries built their own systems — GLONASS, Galileo, BeiDou, and NavIC — creating a truly global network. Today, satellite navigation is used everywhere, from smartphones to airplanes, guiding people and machines across the world with pinpoint accuracy.
