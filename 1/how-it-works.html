How the Telegraph Works

A telegraph system uses a simple electrical circuit that connects two stations by wire. When the operator presses a switch, called a key, it completes the circuit and sends a pulse of electricity through the wire.

Operators tap dots and dashes to send letters using Morse code.

The receiver on the other end detects these pulses and makes clicking sounds. Operators learn to interpret the sound patterns as letters and words. Later versions printed the dots and dashes on paper.

A telegraph sounder converts electric pulses into clicks that represent dots and dashes.

Complex networks of telegraph lines allowed messages to be relayed across countries and even under oceans, making it possible for people thousands of miles apart to communicate in near real time.

Telegraph lines carried messages across long distances using wooden poles and insulated copper wire.
